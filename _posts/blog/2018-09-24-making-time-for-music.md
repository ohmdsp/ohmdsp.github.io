If you are like me, you love to play at least one musicical instrument. It could be the guitar, the piano, the violin, or some other wonderful noise making device. Maybe you have always enjoyed playing, or maybe you are returning to your instrument after years of absence due to the demands of a career, raising children, or just keeping up with life! If you are thinking of starting an instrument for the first time, congratulations! It is a wonderful journey. 
<br><br>
It does not matter how old, or young, you are. If you have been bitten by the music bug, you also understand that the journey to become a better musician takes time. One of the first things you're taught at lessons is that regular practice is required. Sounds easy enough, but balancing all the distractions responsibilities, and temptations in life can make regular practice challenging to implement with any long lasting commitment.

For adults, it can be extra challenging to setup a regular time to play music. Often, there will be influences or people around you that consider practicing to be a low priority, or purely an indulgence. You might ask yourself, "why is it that kids are expected, and often required, to practice every day, but when an adult wants to develop a musical talent it ranks lower in priority than participating in a fantasy football league or washing the car on Saturday's?" Just google for images using the phrase "practicing a musical instrument" and compare how many images are returned with kids practicing versus adults practicing. Culturally, we admire those that are masters of their craft, but we stereotype practicing as something only done during our youth. No wonder kids often dislike practicing a musical instrument and can't wait until their parents let them quit. It's difficult to place value on something they never see their parents doing!
<br><br>
Even when blessed with extra talent, there is only one way for us mere mortals to get good at something like playing an instrument. Consistent effort repeated over a long period of time. Finding good teachers and instruction is also key. The goal of this article is not to tell you how to practice, or how often to practice. However, I do hope to give you some inspiration and an awareness that it is ok to make it a priority.
<br><br>
It can be energizing to connect with a wider community of like-minded musicians. Similar to sports or other activities, music is more fun when shared with other people. Connecting with other musicians is important and will give you ideas and motivation you cannot find alone. You do not need to be a professional musician in order to communicate the language of music with others. In addition, engaging in music is not just about putting your hands on an instrument. It also involves learning about other musicians, playing styles, resources and technology. Sometimes, it will help to hear something new, learn about a new band, or hear the perspective of another person.
For inpiration, let me share a few music related discoveries from around the internet - <br><br>
[MusicPlayer Forums](http://forums.musicplayer.com) is a good resource for connecting with other musicians around the world. The keyboard forum is especially active and you will discover some world-class musicians and session players participating in the conversations. There is a wealth of information available, so read and then ask questions.<br>
The [Gear Page](https://www.thegearpage.net/board/index.php) is one of my favorite guitar related forums. There are reviews and information on just about anything you want to know about in the guitar world. It can also be a great resource for meeting other musicians and gear enthusiasts. If you are shopping for used gear, make sure you browse the buy-and-sell section.
Make sure you wander outside the forums and learn about intereting musicians. They will often teach you from their experiences. For example, Reese Wynans is a legendary keyboard player that has played on many famous recordings and with bands such as Stevie Ray Vaughan's Double Trouble. He is currently playing with Joe Bonamassa's band and I highly recommend the concert DVD/Bluray Joe Bonamassa: Muddy Wolf at Red Rocks. Guitar players often get the limelight, but Reese is both an amazing player and an amazing person. Check out this and other podcasts by [Music Makers and Soul Shakers](https://soundcloud.com/makers-and-shakers).


Booker T. Jones is best known for his band Booker T and The MG's. He was 17 years old when he recorded "Green Onions." If you have not taken the time to learn to play Green Onions, I highly recommend it. Not only is it a fun song to play solo, it is a great song to jam with a band. This NPR Tiny Desk concert is amazing for its simplicity and for providing a pure look at the playing style of Booker T.

If you are interested in learning to play the guitar, then I can think of no better internet resource for getting you started than [Justin Guitar](http://justinguitar.com). Justin Sandercoe is an Australian guitarist that has been offering free lessons online since 2003. His site is full of amazing resources, and he is genuinely a super nice guy. I have purchased some of the DVD's and song books from his site to complement all the amazing material he provides for free. I'm a keyboard player, but started learning the guitar about five years ago. Thankfully, justinguitar.com was one of my first experiences.
<br><br>
How about learning to play jazz? I recently started a journey into jazz by combining lessons from my regular piano teacher with lessons from [Peter Martin on the Open Studio platform](https://www.openstudionetwork.com/project/peter-martin/). These on-demand lessons are fantastic and challenging. Having affordable access to world-renouned musicians for private lessons is a real luxury the internet provides. Take advantage of it!
<br><br>
Speaking of world-class musicians giving lessons. Check out [Artist Works](https://artistworks.com) and sign up for some flatpick guitar lessons with [Bryan Sutton](https://www.bryansutton.com)! These lessons are amazing resources and you will get personalized feedback directly from the instructor. Don't miss all the other instrument lessons available on this site. Artist Works has regular sales on lessons so sign up for notifications if you want to study from multiple instructors.
<br><br>
How many of you are composers? Growing up, I wanted to be a film composer. I loved the movies with scores from Jerry Goldsmith, John Williams, Ennio Morricone, James Newton Howard, Alan Silvestri, and so many more. I would drool at the magazine images of their studios - packed with synthesizers and gear. Nowdays, the equipment needed to compose for film, TV and games is more accessible. Most composers utilize sample libraries for mocking up scores or even delivering final music. One of my favorite sample library companies is [Sptitfire Audio](https://www.spitfireaudio.com) in the UK. The co-founder, [Christian Henson has a YouTube channel](https://www.youtube.com/channel/UCXCXxhRVYvBOX45_gxr0iHA) packed full of resources and advice for composers. Aside from being a very talented composer, Christian is a gifted teacher and a direct and honest person. You can find a mountain of inspiration from the long list of videos available on his channel.
<br><br>
You may find that there is not enough time in the day to take advantage of all the resources available for learning to play music. For now, just pick something interesting and set aside some protected time for regular practice and learning. Find some other musicians that share your interest and priorities. You may need to look outside your existing social circle and make some new contacts. It will be fun! In a future post, I will dive deeper into some hot topics like Artificial Intelligence (AI) for composing. Until then…enjoy the music!
